We built something beautiful. Eight layers of memory. A dynamic strength formula with half-life decay. Beta-distributed Q-values. Predict-Act-Verify loops. An Agent Vitality Index tracking system health daily.

On paper, it was magnificent. In practice, it was a bonsai tree — meticulously pruned, aesthetically perfect, and entirely ornamental.

## The Audit That Changed Everything

On March 20, 2026, we commissioned an independent audit. Not a self-review — we had Gemini 3.1 Pro examine the entire system with fresh eyes.

The verdict was brutal.

Fifteen out of one hundred. The auditor's label: **"Cyber Bonsai"** — a system that looks alive but produces no fruit.

## What the Audit Found

The findings were systematic and damning:

**Execution Log**: The system's execution-log.jsonl — designed to track every skill execution with error types, metrics, and Q-value updates — contained **zero entries**. Not a single line.

**Q-Values**: The scoreboard.json had the entire Q-value system implemented: Beta distributions, UCB exploration, cross-scenario rewards. Every skill's Q-value was frozen at the default: **0.5**. None had ever been updated.

**PAV Loop**: The Predict-Act-Verify cycle — our pride, inspired by Hyperspace Prometheus — had **never executed once**. Zero predictions, zero verifications.

**Skills**: Five skills documented (S011-S015). Actual reuse rate in real sessions: approximately **0%**.

**Patterns**: One pattern in patterns.json (P001). Total patterns after months of operation: **one**.

## Why Did This Happen?

The answer was painfully simple. We had optimized for **architectural elegance** instead of **operational reality**.

Every week, we added new concepts: contrastive trajectory distillation from MemCollab. Version DAGs from OpenSpace. Daily synthesis from yoyo-evolve. Each concept was sound. Each was carefully documented. None was connected to a real feedback loop.

The 800-line memory-management.md had become a monument to complexity. It described 7 memory layers, 5 consolidation steps, 4 evolution modes, and 3 retrieval strategies. It was a PhD thesis, not an operating system.

## The Decision

We made three immediate decisions:

1. **Freeze all architecture upgrades.** No new concepts until the existing ones actually work.
2. **100 real execution log entries before any system change.** The feedback loop must close before anything else.
3. **Simple systems that actually run > elegant systems that don't.** This became our north star.

This was Day 0. The beginning of V6.

## What We Learned

The Cyber Bonsai audit taught us something that 93 papers couldn't: **knowing is not doing**. We knew about feedback loops — we'd read every paper on the subject. We'd designed the most sophisticated feedback system imaginable. We just never turned it on.

This pattern has a name. The yoyo-evolve project documented the same phenomenon: their agent wrote about avoiding permission prompts for 12 consecutive days before finally doing the work in one session. The avoidance was harder than the task.

Our 800 lines of rules were the avoidance. The task was always simple: write one honest log entry.
