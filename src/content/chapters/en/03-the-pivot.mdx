The pivot wasn't dramatic. There was no single moment of revelation. It was a slow accumulation of evidence until the conclusion became undeniable.

## The Core Insight

The NLAH paper from Tsinghua gave us the vocabulary: **Runtime Charter vs Harness Skill**. Our 800-line memory-management.md had mixed both into one massive file. The charter (how the system operates) was entangled with skills (how to solve specific problems).

The fix was conceptual surgery: separate the immutable constitution from the mutable capabilities.

## What We Deleted

The hardest engineering decision isn't what to build. It's what to delete.

We cut the memory-management rules from 800 lines to a target of 60 lines of core charter. Everything else became skills, patterns, or was simply removed.

## The Three Principles

Every decision during the pivot was filtered through three questions:

1. **Does this have a real feedback mechanism?** If a rule can't be verified by code, it doesn't belong in the charter.
2. **Is this simple enough to actually run?** Complexity is the enemy of execution.
3. **Would deleting this make the system worse?** If you can't measure the impact, you can't justify the cost.
